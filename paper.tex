\begin{abstract}
\noindent Whether the supra-competitive prices reached by pricing algorithms reflect genuine tacit
collusion or a spurious learning artifact is central to competition policy. I study this in homogeneous-good
Bertrand competition---the environment most hostile to collusion---contrasting a stateful
$\varepsilon$-greedy Q-learner with a stateless mean-based bandit, and classifying outcomes with a
model-free, convergence-validated Shannon-entropy diagnostic. Three results emerge. First, the stateless
learner reaches supra-competitive prices immediately and in $90$--$96\%$ of runs, but only weakly (collusion
index $\Delta\approx0.13$), whereas the stateful Q-learner converges to competitive pricing at plausible
horizons (only $2\%$ collusive, despite $\approx 90\%$ of runs converging) and learns to collude only
gradually as the horizon lengthens (to $50\%$ at $10^6$ periods), but intensely ($\Delta$ rising to $0.46$).
Agent memory, not sophistication, is thus the operative margin, generating a reliability-versus-intensity
trade-off. Second, a forced-deviation experiment shows the two coordinations differ in kind: the stateless
learner is structurally unable to punish (rival response amplitude $0.00$), so its pricing is spurious,
whereas the Q-learner punishes deviations sharply ($5.61\!\to\!4.05$ against a $0.45$ no-deviation band), so
its coordination is genuine. Third, because the stateless learner converges, its advantage is not the
non-convergence artifact often raised against tabular results. The policy reading is detection-centred: a
model-free, algorithm-agnostic screen must flag supra-competitive pricing, because the simplest learner
coordinates most reliably and least detectably.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} algorithmic collusion; reinforcement learning; agent memory; entropy;
emergent coordination; competition policy. \quad \textbf{JEL:} C63, C73, D43, D83, L13, L41.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The delegation of pricing to autonomous algorithms has made one question first-order for competition
authorities: when such algorithms charge supra-competitive prices, is this genuine tacit collusion that an
effects-based law should reach, or a spurious learning artifact of no cartel significance? Empirically the
stakes are real---\citet{assad2024algorithmic} find that algorithmic-pricing adoption in German retail
gasoline raised margins by roughly $28\%$. Theoretically the question is sharp: \citet{calvano2020artificial}
show that Q-learning agents in \emph{differentiated}-good markets learn reward--punishment-supported
collusion, while a critical literature argues that much apparent algorithmic collusion is an under-exploration
``mistake'' that vanishes with more exploration or longer horizons
\citep{abadalambin2023,abada2024collusion,denboermeylahnschinkel2024}, and that whether learning escapes the
competitive equilibrium turns on what agents \emph{condition on} rather than on raw sophistication
\citep{askerfershtmanpakes2024,possnig2023reinforcement}.

I take this debate to the hardest case---\emph{homogeneous}-good Bertrand competition, where the static
equilibrium is competitive and the temptation to undercut is undiluted by product differentiation---and
make the comparison turn on a single primitive: agent \emph{memory}. I contrast a \emph{stateful}
$\varepsilon$-greedy Q-learner, whose state is the rival's previous price (so it \emph{can} condition on,
and punish, deviations), against a \emph{stateless} mean-based bandit, which values each price by its
historical average payoff and cannot condition on anything. I classify every run with a model-free
Shannon-entropy diagnostic into competitive, collusive, or chaotic regimes, and---crucially---I validate
convergence \emph{non-circularly} (by greedy-policy stability, not by low entropy) so that the comparison is
not confounded by one learner simply failing to settle.

My findings (Section~\ref{sec:results}) overturn the natural ``sophistication drives collusion'' reading
and replace it with a memory-based one.

\textbf{(1) Reliability versus intensity.} The stateless learner reaches supra-competitive prices in
$90$--$96\%$ of runs and does so immediately, but \emph{weakly}: its collusion index is $\Delta\approx0.13$
(on a $[0,1]$ scale from the discrete Bertrand--Nash benchmark to joint monopoly). The stateful Q-learner,
at economically plausible horizons, instead \emph{converges to competition}---only $2\%$ of runs collude
even though $89\%$ converge---and learns to collude only slowly as the horizon grows ($0\%$, $25\%$, $44\%$,
$50\%$ at $T=3\times10^4$ to $10^6$), but when it does, its collusion is \emph{intense} ($\Delta$ rising to
$0.46$). Memory, not sophistication, is the operative margin.

\textbf{(2) Spurious versus genuine.} A frozen-policy forced-deviation experiment shows the two
coordinations are different in kind. The stateless learner, being unable to condition on the rival, does
not respond to a deviation at all (rival price response amplitude $0.00$): its supra-competitive pricing is
\emph{structurally spurious}, in the sense of \citet{calvano2023genuine} and the ``spontaneous coupling'' of
\citet{banchiomantegazza2023}. The Q-learner punishes a deviation sharply---the rival's price falls from
$5.61$ to $4.05$, against a no-deviation noise band of only $0.45$---so its coordination is \emph{genuine},
reward--punishment-supported tacit collusion.

\textbf{(3) The convergence-artifact critique does not apply.} Because the stateless learner
\emph{converges} (it is fully policy-stable) and still colludes, its advantage cannot be dismissed as a
failure to learn---the standard objection to tabular results
\citep{denboermeylahnschinkel2024,abada2024collusion}. I further trace the horizon dependence of the
Q-learner directly, to $10^6$ periods, rather than asserting convergence.

The policy implication is detection-centred. Both spurious and genuine supra-competitive pricing raise
prices and harm no one's ability to charge them; yet the spurious variety is produced by the
\emph{simplest} learner, arises \emph{fastest}, and---lacking any punishment dynamics---is the hardest to
distinguish from competition by behavioural signatures. A model-free screen that flags supra-competitive
\emph{outcomes}, independent of the firm's algorithm, is therefore the appropriate instrument; my entropy
diagnostic is one such screen. The remainder of the paper presents the model (Section~\ref{sec:model}), the
learners (Section~\ref{sec:algos}), the diagnostic (Section~\ref{sec:diag}), the results
(Section~\ref{sec:results}), and a discussion of scope and limitations (Sections~\ref{sec:disc}--\ref{sec:conc}).

\section{Related work}\label{sec:lit}
My study sits within the algorithmic-collusion literature founded by \citet{calvano2020artificial} and
\citet{klein2021autonomous}, who establish that Q-learning agents autonomously learn
reward--punishment-supported supra-competitive pricing in differentiated-product and sequential Bertrand
settings respectively. I depart from this canon in three ways: I study the harder homogeneous-good case on
a coarse memory-one grid; I add a model-free, convergence-validated entropy classifier that sorts runs
into competitive, collusive, and chaotic regimes; and I contrast a stateful Q-learner against a stateless
mean-based bandit. The closest and most demanding neighbours warn that supra-competitive pricing may be an
artifact rather than strategy. \citet{abadalambin2023} and \citet{abada2024collusion} trace it to imperfect
exploration and under-convergence (``collusion by mistake''); \citet{denboermeylahnschinkel2024} argue
Q-learning collusion is fragile and emerges only on economically irrelevant timescales. Rather than contest
these critiques, I adopt them as my methodological baseline---conditioning every collusion statistic on
validated convergence over horizons up to $10^6$ periods---and then draw two distinctions they do not.
First, reliability versus intensity: the stateless bandit reaches supra-competitive prices more often but at
lower normalized collusion index, while the Q-learner colludes less often but harder. Second, a
genuine-versus-spurious test indexed by learner class: a forced-deviation diagnostic shows the bandit's
coordination---consistent with the ``spontaneous coupling'' of \citet{banchiomantegazza2023} and the
spurious category of \citet{calvano2023genuine}---is structurally non-punishable, whereas converged
Q-learning is reward--punishment-supported. Crucially, my mean-based result is \emph{not} refuted by
\citet{hartlinelongzhang2024}: their theorem shows external/mean-based no-regret learning is
\emph{insufficient} for competition and can permit supra-competitive prices---it never implies mean-based
learners become competitive. My finding is a concrete, finite-horizon, tabular, $\varepsilon$-greedy
instantiation of exactly that permitted regime, and \citet{dolgopolov2024reinforcement}'s stateless-defection
theorem applies to my bandit, not to my memory-enabled Q-learner. I thereby complement
\citet{askerfershtmanpakes2024} and \citet{possnig2023reinforcement} by isolating learner \emph{memory},
not update information, as the operative margin. My regime taxonomy is in the spirit of the evolutionary
oligopoly dynamics studied in this journal by \citet{bischilamantia2022}, though my coordination is
emergent from learning rather than hard-wired into agent types.

\section{Model}\label{sec:model}
Two firms $i\in\{1,2\}$ play a repeated Bertrand game with a homogeneous good. Each period each firm chooses
a price from a finite evenly spaced grid
\[
\mathcal P=\{p_{\min},\,p_{\min}+\Delta,\dots,p_{\max}\},\qquad \Delta=\tfrac{p_{\max}-p_{\min}}{K-1},
\]
with $p_{\min}=0$, $p_{\max}=10$, and $K$ price points; I use $K=21$ as the baseline and $K=7$ as a
coarse-grid contrast, and treat $K$ as an object of study. Marginal cost is $c=1$. Demand is inelastic and
normalised to one unit, allocated by the Bertrand rule: the lowest-priced firm serves the unit (ties split),
so firm $i$'s profit is $\pi_i=(p_i-c)\,q_i$ with $q_i=\mathbf 1[p_i=\min_j p_j]/|\arg\min_j p_j|$. Firms
interact for $T$ periods (baseline $T=3\times10^4$; varied to $10^6$).

\paragraph{The discrete competitive benchmark.} On a discrete grid the static Bertrand--Nash outcome has
both firms at the lowest grid price weakly above cost, which earns a small but \emph{positive} per-firm
profit unless a grid point coincides with $c$. I therefore anchor the collusion index on the true discrete
benchmark $\pi^N=(p^{\mathrm{comp}}-c)/2$ with $p^{\mathrm{comp}}=\min\{p\in\mathcal P:p\ge c\}$
(so $\pi^N=0.33$ at $K=7$ and $\pi^N=0$ at $K=21$, where $p=1$ lies on the grid), and on joint monopoly
$\pi^M=(p_{\max}-c)/2=4.5$. The \emph{collusion index} of a run is
\begin{equation}
\Delta=\frac{\bar\pi-\pi^N}{\pi^M-\pi^N}\in[0,1],
\label{eq:delta}
\end{equation}
where $\bar\pi$ is mean per-firm profit over the final $T_0=2000$ periods; $\Delta=0$ is competitive and
$\Delta=1$ is symmetric joint monopoly. This corrects a common mis-anchoring (setting $\pi^N=0$) and I
compute $\Delta$ only on \emph{converged} runs (Section~\ref{sec:diag}).

\section{Learning algorithms}\label{sec:algos}
\paragraph{Stateful Q-learning.} Firm $i$ keeps a table $Q_i(s,a)$, with state $s_t$ equal to the rival's
\emph{previous} price; it plays $\varepsilon$-greedily and updates
\begin{equation}
Q_i(s_t,a_t)\leftarrow Q_i(s_t,a_t)+\alpha\!\left[\pi_{i,t}+\gamma\max_{a'}Q_i(s_{t+1},a')-Q_i(s_t,a_t)\right],
\label{eq:q}
\end{equation}
with $\alpha=0.10$, $\gamma=0.95$, and annealed exploration $\varepsilon_t=\varepsilon_0/(1+\delta t)$,
$\varepsilon_0=0.10$, $\delta=3\times10^{-4}$ \citep{watkins1992qlearning}. Conditioning on $s_t$ is what
allows reactive, punishment-based strategies.

\paragraph{Stateless mean-based learning.} The benchmark keeps the running average payoff $M_i(p)$ of each
price and plays $\varepsilon$-greedily with respect to it---a multi-armed bandit with no state, no discount,
and no temporal-difference bootstrap. It cannot condition on the rival, so it cannot punish. To rule out an
exploration-schedule confound I run both learners under \emph{identical} schedules (Section~\ref{sec:results}).

\section{A convergence-validated entropy diagnostic}\label{sec:diag}
I classify outcomes from action logs alone. Over the final $T_0$ periods I compute each firm's
$K$-normalised Shannon entropy $H_i=-\!\sum_p f_i(p)\log_2 f_i(p)/\log_2 K\in[0,1]$ with a Miller--Madow
bias correction, and average to $\bar H$; let $\bar p$ be the mean price. A run is \emph{competitive} if
$\bar H<H^\ast$ and $\bar p<2c$, \emph{collusive} if $\bar H<H^\ast$ and $\bar p\ge 2c$, and \emph{chaotic}
if $\bar H\ge H^\ast$. Rather than assert a threshold, I \emph{derive} $H^\ast$ as the antimode of the
pooled $\bar H$ distribution, obtaining $H^\ast=0.288$.

\paragraph{Non-circular convergence.} The standard objection to tabular collusion results is that one
learner has not finished learning. I therefore certify convergence \emph{independently of entropy}: a run
has converged if each firm's greedy policy ($\arg\max_a Q_i(s,\cdot)$ over visited states, or $\arg\max_p
M_i$) is unchanged over the final $10\%$ of the run. All collusion statistics below are reported both
unconditionally and on converged runs.

\paragraph{Honesty about thresholds and redundancy.} The collusive share is sensitive to $H^\ast$ (it ranges
from $46\%$ at $H^\ast=0.20$ to $97\%$ at $H^\ast=0.80$ in the pooled sample), so I do not claim threshold
robustness; I report the data-derived value and the full sensitivity (Figure~\ref{fig:classifier}, right).
The diagnostic is \emph{not} redundant with $\Delta$: entropy separates converged from chaotic play, which a
price- or profit-based index cannot (Figure~\ref{fig:classifier}, left).

\caption{Left: the $(\bar H,\Delta)$ plane---entropy is non-redundant with the collusion index, separating

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}
Unless noted, each cell is $100$ independent seeded runs at the baseline ($K=21$, $T=3\times10^4$).

\subsection{Reliability versus intensity, and the death of the artifact critique}
Table~\ref{tab:baseline} reports the core comparison. At the baseline grid the stateless learner is
collusive in $94\%$ of runs but only weakly ($\Delta=0.14$), while the Q-learner is collusive in just $2\%$
of runs---\emph{despite} $89\%$ of its runs converging. The Q-learner therefore does not fail to learn; it
\emph{learns to compete}. Restricting to converged runs barely moves the figures ($93\%$ vs.\ $2\%$),
so the gap is not a non-convergence artifact (Figure~\ref{fig:conv}). On the coarse $K=7$ grid the Q-learner
colludes far more ($52\%$; $67\%$ among converged) at higher intensity ($\Delta=0.41$), confirming that grid
resolution---how finely the learner can approximate the competitive best response---is itself decisive.

\subsection{Robustness to the exploration schedule}
The contrast is not an artifact of annealing. Under \emph{identical} schedules the mean-based learner
dominates throughout: with annealed exploration the collusive shares are $94\%$ (mean) vs.\ $2\%$ (Q), and
with constant $\varepsilon=0.10$ they are $86\%$ vs.\ $0\%$. Holding the schedule fixed isolates memory,
not exploration, as the driver.

\subsection{The horizon ladder: does the Q-learner catch up?}
Because the critical literature stresses that Q-learning collusion may need economically irrelevant
horizons, I trace the comparison to $T=10^6$ at $K=21$ (Table~\ref{tab:ladder}, Figure~\ref{fig:ladder}).
The mean-based share is essentially flat and high ($96\%\!\to\!90\%$) with stable, weak intensity
($\Delta\approx0.13$). The Q-learner's collusive share rises monotonically from $0\%$ to $50\%$, and its
intensity rises with it ($\Delta:0.32\!\to\!0.46$): given enough time the stateful learner increasingly
discovers collusion, and the collusion it discovers is \emph{strong}. But even at $10^6$ periods the gap
does not close, and---as the next subsection shows---the two coordinations remain different in kind
throughout.

\subsection{Genuine versus spurious: the forced-deviation test}
The decisive distinction is mechanistic. I take converged collusive runs, freeze the learned policy, force
one firm to undercut for a single period, and trace the rival's response, comparing it to a no-deviation
counterfactual with bootstrap bands (Figure~\ref{fig:impulse}). For the Q-learner the rival punishes
sharply: its price falls from a pre-deviation $5.61$ to a trough of $4.05$---a drop of $1.56$, more than
three times the no-deviation band amplitude of $0.45$---before cooperation recovers. For the mean-based
learner the rival's response amplitude is exactly $0.00$: being stateless, it cannot condition on the
deviation and does not react. The stateless learner's supra-competitive pricing is therefore
\emph{structurally spurious}; the Q-learner's is \emph{genuine}, reward--punishment-supported collusion.

\caption{Forced-deviation response (frozen policy). Q-learning (left): the rival punishes a deviation well
beyond the no-deviation band. Mean-based (right): no response---spurious coordination.}
\label{fig:impulse}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Patience raises intensity, not reliability}
Finally, the role of the discount factor differs from the folk-theorem intuition once memory and grid are
handled correctly. At $K=21$, increasing $\gamma$ from $0.20$ to $0.95$ \emph{lowers} the Q-learner's
collusive share ($46\%\!\to\!26\%\!\to\!6\%\!\to\!2\%$) while \emph{raising} its intensity on the runs that
do collude ($\Delta:0.21\!\to\!0.32\!\to\!0.35\!\to\!0.35$): patient learners more reliably find the
competitive equilibrium, but when they do collude they sustain it harder (Figure~\ref{fig:gamma}). I report
share and intensity separately rather than conflating them into a single ``patience drives collusion''
claim.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}
Three implications follow. First, in the homogeneous-good limit the relevant primitive is agent
\emph{memory}, not sophistication: the learner that \emph{cannot} react coordinates most reliably (but
spuriously and weakly), while the learner that \emph{can} react competes by default and colludes only
slowly (but genuinely and strongly). This reconciles the apparently opposed findings that ``simple learners
collude'' and that ``Q-learning collusion is fragile/slow''---they describe the two faces of the same
memory margin. Second, the genuine/spurious distinction is \emph{operational and structural}, not a matter
of exploration tuning: a stateless learner cannot punish at any $\varepsilon$, so its coordination is
spurious by construction, which the forced-deviation test verifies. Third, the convergence-artifact critique
that dominates this literature does not apply to the stateless result, because that learner converges; it
applies, correctly, to the stateful learner, whose horizon dependence I therefore trace explicitly.

For enforcement, the reading is detection-centred and, I think, uncomfortable. The coordination that is
easiest to produce (it needs no memory, no design, and no patience), arises fastest, and is hardest to
distinguish from competition by reaction-based signatures is precisely the \emph{spurious} kind generated by
the simplest algorithm. A behavioural test for punishment dynamics would clear the stateless learner while
catching the Q-learner, exactly inverting their consumer impact at short horizons. An effects-based screen on
supra-competitive \emph{outcomes}---of which my model-free entropy diagnostic is an example---is the
appropriate instrument, because it is agnostic to the algorithm and to whether the coordination is genuine.

\section{Limitations and conclusion}\label{sec:conc}
The analysis is computational and offers no convergence proofs; multi-agent Q-learning may cycle rather than
converge \citep{waltmankaymak2008}, which my chaotic regime and the falling convergence rates at long
horizons reflect. I study homogeneous goods, inelastic unit demand, tabular memory-one learners, and a
single market game. Inelastic demand is deliberate---it isolates the coordination mechanism---but it implies
zero deadweight loss, so my policy claims concern \emph{detection of supra-competitive pricing}, not
welfare magnitudes; an elastic-demand extension is the natural next step, as is replication across Cournot
and repeated-game environments to test whether the memory margin is invariant to best-response geometry.
Reported regime frequencies depend on the entropy threshold, which I therefore derive from the data and
whose sensitivity I report in full.

Subject to these caveats, the picture is clear and honest. In the environment most hostile to collusion, a
stateless learner reaches supra-competitive prices immediately, reliably, weakly, and spuriously, while a
stateful learner competes by default and learns intense, genuine collusion only slowly. Memory is the
margin; reliability and intensity trade off; and because the most reliable coordination is the most spurious
and least detectable, the case for outcome-based, algorithm-agnostic screening is strengthened rather than
weakened.
