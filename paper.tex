\begin{abstract}
\noindent As firms delegate pricing to autonomous algorithms, whether such algorithms learn to sustain
supra-competitive prices without instruction has become a first-order question for competition policy. I
study this question in the environment least hospitable to it: repeated Bertrand competition with a
\emph{homogeneous} good, where classical theory predicts marginal-cost pricing from a mere duopoly. Using
faithful $\varepsilon$-greedy Q-learning---with a temporal-difference update, a discount factor, and state
given by rivals' previous prices---together with a stateless mean-based (bandit) benchmark, I run $100$
independent simulations per configuration and classify outcomes by a Shannon-entropy measure of price
dispersion into competitive, collusive, and chaotic regimes. I report four results. (i) Supra-competitive
collusion does arise even with homogeneous goods---in $56\%$ of baseline duopoly runs, at roughly half the
monopoly markup ($\Delta\approx0.43$)---but it is \emph{conditional}: the remaining runs fail to converge
(chaotic), and collusion requires a tractable action space, annealed exploration, and a long horizon.
(ii) Contrary to the hypothesis that sophistication drives collusion, the simpler mean-based bandit
colludes significantly more often than Q-learning ($89\%$ vs.\ $56\%$; two-proportion $z=5.23$,
$p<10^{-6}$). (iii) A frozen-policy deviation experiment recovers a systematic, if modest,
reward--punishment response: a forced undercut triggers a price cut by the rival followed by recovery to
the collusive level. (iv) The intensity of collusion rises monotonically with patience, the comparative
static predicted by the folk theorem ($\Delta$ from $0.23$ at $\gamma=0.2$ to $0.45$ at $\gamma=0.95$), and
collusion collapses as the grid fines, as exploration persists, as the horizon shortens, and as a third
firm enters. The picture that emerges both broadens and bounds the algorithmic-collusion concern: tacit
coordination requires no sophisticated learning, yet in the hardest competitive environment it survives
only on a knife-edge of conditions, several of which are observable and hence regulable.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} algorithmic collusion; Bertrand competition; reinforcement learning;
Q-learning; tacit collusion; competition policy.\quad\textbf{JEL:} D43, L13, L41, C63.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
The Bertrand model delivers one of the sharpest predictions in economics: when firms sell a homogeneous
good at a common constant marginal cost, the unique Nash equilibrium has every firm pricing at marginal
cost and earning zero profit, so that even a duopoly reproduces the competitive outcome \citep{tirole1988}.
This \emph{Bertrand paradox} is routinely relaxed in theory by product differentiation \citep{hotelling1929},
capacity constraints \citep{edgeworth1925}, and repeated interaction supporting tacit collusion
\citep{ivaldi2003}. The rise of algorithmic pricing introduces a different channel: as firms delegate
pricing to autonomous learning algorithms, those algorithms may \emph{learn} to price above cost without
being programmed or instructed to collude---a possibility that has become a first-order concern for
competition authorities \citep{harrington2018,ezrachi2016}.

The influential study of \citet{calvano2020} showed, in a \emph{differentiated}-product duopoly, that
$Q$-learning agents converge to supra-competitive prices and sustain them with reward--punishment
strategies resembling those of repeated-game theory. I ask whether this phenomenon survives in the
\emph{homogeneous}-good environment, where the competitive force is strongest and the Bertrand paradox
binds. The choice of environment is deliberate and consequential: collusion ``even here'' would be a
stronger and more alarming finding, whereas its failure or fragility here would sharply delimit the scope
of the algorithmic-collusion concern.

I implement two learning rules in a repeated homogeneous-good Bertrand game. The first is faithful
$\varepsilon$-greedy $Q$-learning, with a temporal-difference update (equation~\eqref{eq:q}), a discount
factor $\gamma$, and a state equal to rivals' prices in the previous period. The second is a stateless
mean-based learner---a multi-armed bandit that values each price by its historical average payoff, without
discounting, state, or temporal-difference bootstrapping. Comparing the two isolates the contribution of
$Q$-learning's additional machinery. For each parameter configuration I run $100$ independent simulations,
classify each by a Shannon-entropy measure of price dispersion into one of three regimes
(competitive, collusive, chaotic), and summarise the degree of coordination by a normalised collusion
index $\Delta\in[0,1]$ running from Bertrand--Nash ($0$) to symmetric joint monopoly ($1$).

\paragraph{Findings.} My results revise, rather than echo, the differentiated-good literature.

\emph{First, collusion is real but conditional.} Under the baseline duopoly, $56\%$ ($\pm5.0$) of runs are
collusive at a mean index $\Delta\approx0.43$---about half-way to joint monopoly---while $44\%$ fail to
converge and are chaotic; competitive convergence essentially never occurs. Collusion is not the universal
outcome the differentiated-good reading might suggest; in the homogeneous limit, learning to collude and
failing to settle are comparably likely.

\emph{Second, sophistication is not the driver.} The simpler mean-based bandit colludes in $89\%$ of runs
against only $56\%$ for $Q$-learning, a gap that is large and statistically decisive (two-proportion
$z=5.23$, $p=1.7\times10^{-7}$). $Q$-learning's temporal-difference bootstrap and enlarged state space make
convergence \emph{harder}, not easier, so a larger share of its runs end chaotic. Supra-competitive
coordination is therefore a generic tendency of independent payoff maximisation, not an artefact of
advanced reinforcement learning.

\emph{Third, the punishment mechanism is present but modest.} Freezing a converged collusive policy and
forcing a one-period undercut, I observe the rival cut its price in response and then return to the
collusive level---a reward--punishment-and-recovery pattern, though the punishment is shallow.

\emph{Fourth, the comparative statics are sharp and theory-consistent.} Collusion intensity rises
monotonically with the discount factor ($\Delta:0.23\to0.45$ as $\gamma:0.2\to0.95$), the folk-theorem
patience channel; and the collusive share falls monotonically as the price grid fines ($57\%\to3\%$), as
exploration persists ($57\%\to0\%$), as the horizon shortens ($57\%\to10\%$), and as a third firm enters
($57\%\to0\%$).

Taken together, the algorithmic-collusion concern is both broadened and bounded. It is broadened because
coordination requires no clever algorithm: a naive bandit suffices, and in fact coordinates more reliably.
It is bounded because, in the homogeneous-good environment, collusion is fragile and condition-dependent,
so the differentiated-good results of \citet{calvano2020} are best read as an upper bound on how readily
algorithmic collusion arises. Encouragingly for enforcement, several of the conditions that sustain
collusion---persistence of exploration, market structure, the coarseness of admissible price grids---are
observable, and hence candidate levers for intervention.

\section{Related Literature}\label{sec:lit}
The paper connects three strands. The first is classical Bertrand theory: the paradox of
\citet{bertrand1883} and \citet{tirole1988}, and its relaxations through differentiation
\citep{hotelling1929}, capacity \citep{edgeworth1925}, and repeated interaction \citep{ivaldi2003}. I add
adaptive algorithmic learning as a further channel, and---unlike the classical relaxations---find it
operative only conditionally in the homogeneous case.

The second strand is the algorithmic-collusion literature. \citet{calvano2020} is the closest antecedent:
$Q$-learning collusion with differentiated products and reward--punishment strategies. \citet{klein2021}
establishes collusion under sequential (rather than simultaneous) pricing; \citet{brown2023} study
competition between pricing algorithms from a design perspective; and \citet{assad2024} provide empirical
evidence from retail gasoline that algorithmic adoption raised margins. Relative to this work, my
contribution is threefold: I push the analysis to the homogeneous-good limit, I provide an
entropy-based regime classifier, and I document that the \emph{simpler} learner colludes more---reframing
the phenomenon as independent of algorithmic sophistication.

The third strand is learning in games. \citet{watkins1992} introduce $Q$-learning; \citet{waltman2008}
show that multi-agent $Q$-learning may cycle rather than converge---precisely the chaotic regime I find
to be dominant under fine grids and short horizons. My results are consistent with this non-convergence
being the default, and collusion the exception, in the homogeneous environment.

\section{Model}\label{sec:model}
I consider a repeated Bertrand pricing game with $n\in\{2,3\}$ firms producing a homogeneous good. Each
firm $i$ chooses a price $p_i$ from a finite, evenly spaced grid
\[
\mathcal P=\{p_{\min},\,p_{\min}+\Delta,\,\dots,\,p_{\max}\},\qquad
\Delta=\frac{p_{\max}-p_{\min}}{K-1},\quad K=|\mathcal P|,
\]
with $p_{\min}=0$ and $p_{\max}=10$. The baseline uses $K=7$ price points; Section~\ref{sec:robust} varies
$K$ up to $41$ and shows that the choice of granularity is itself a key determinant of outcomes. All firms
share constant marginal cost $c=1$. Demand is inelastic and normalised to a single unit, allocated by the
standard Bertrand rule under which the lowest-priced firm(s) split the market:
\[
q_i(\mathbf p)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{1}{|\{j:p_j=\min_k p_k\}|} & \text{if } p_i=\min_k p_k,\\[1mm]
0 & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\qquad \pi_i(\mathbf p)=(p_i-c)\,q_i(\mathbf p).
\]
Firms interact for $T$ periods (baseline $T=30{,}000$). The following is immediate and fixes the
competitive benchmark.

\begin{proposition}[Stage-game Nash]
In the one-shot game the unique Nash equilibrium (up to grid granularity) has $p_i=c$ for all $i$ and zero
profit.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}[Sketch]
At any common price $p>c$ each firm earns $(p-c)/n>0$ but can deviate to the next-lower grid point, capture
the entire unit of demand, and earn approximately $(p-c)$, which exceeds $(p-c)/n$; hence $p>c$ is not an
equilibrium. Pricing below $c$ yields negative profit whenever the firm wins. The only mutual best response
is $p_i=c$.
\end{proof}

I summarise the degree of collusion in any run by the normalised index
\begin{equation}
\Delta=\frac{\bar\pi-\pi^{N}}{\pi^{M}-\pi^{N}},\qquad \pi^{N}=0,\quad \pi^{M}=\frac{p_{\max}-c}{n},
\label{eq:delta}
\end{equation}
where $\bar\pi$ is the average per-firm per-period profit over the final $T_0=2{,}000$ periods. Thus
$\Delta=0$ corresponds to Bertrand--Nash and $\Delta=1$ to symmetric joint monopoly (all firms at
$p_{\max}$, splitting demand).

\section{Learning Algorithms}\label{sec:algos}
\paragraph{Q-learning.} Each firm runs $\varepsilon$-greedy $Q$-learning \citep{watkins1992}, maintaining a
table $Q^i(s,a)$ of action values. The state $s_t$ is the vector of rivals' prices in the previous period
and the action is the firm's own current price. With probability $1-\varepsilon$ the firm exploits,
$a^i_t=\arg\max_a Q^i(s_t,a)$, and with probability $\varepsilon$ it explores uniformly. After observing
profit $\pi^i_t$ and the new state $s_{t+1}$ it applies the temporal-difference update
\begin{equation}
Q^i(s_t,a^i_t)\;\leftarrow\;Q^i(s_t,a^i_t)+\alpha\Big[\pi^i_t+\gamma\max_{a'}Q^i(s_{t+1},a')-Q^i(s_t,a^i_t)\Big],
\label{eq:q}
\end{equation}
with learning rate $\alpha=0.10$ and discount factor $\gamma=0.95$ at baseline. Exploration is annealed,
$\varepsilon_t=\varepsilon_0/(1+\delta t)$ with $\varepsilon_0=0.10$ and $\delta=3\times10^{-4}$;
Section~\ref{sec:robust} contrasts annealed and constant schedules. Tables are initialised at (numerically)
zero. The two terms that distinguish \eqref{eq:q} from a bandit are the forward-looking continuation value
$\gamma\max_{a'}Q^i(s_{t+1},a')$ and the dependence on $s_t$, which together permit reactive,
state-contingent strategies.

\paragraph{Mean-based benchmark.} The control is stateless mean-based learning: each agent maintains the
running average payoff of each price, $M^i_t(p)$, and chooses $\varepsilon$-greedily with respect to it.
This is a multi-armed bandit with neither discounting, state, nor temporal-difference bootstrap; comparing
it to $Q$-learning isolates the effect of the latter's machinery.

\section{Behavioural Regimes and Entropy Classification}\label{sec:regimes}
Three qualitatively distinct long-run behaviours arise (Figure~\ref{fig:regimes}): a \emph{competitive}
regime with prices near marginal cost and $\Delta\approx0$; a \emph{collusive} regime in which both firms
converge to a common supra-competitive price; and a \emph{chaotic} regime of persistent oscillation without
convergence, the multi-agent cycling anticipated by \citet{waltman2008}.

\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{fig1_regimes.png}
\caption{Representative price trajectories. Left: competitive ($\gamma=0.20$). Centre: collusive
(baseline). Right: chaotic (fine grid, $K=41$). The dashed line is marginal cost $c=1$.}
\label{fig:regimes}
\end{figure}

To classify runs without manual inspection I compute, over the final $T_0$ periods, the empirical price
distribution $f^i(p)$ of each agent and its Shannon entropy $H^i=-\sum_{p}f^i(p)\log_2 f^i(p)$, and average
across agents to $\bar H=\tfrac1n\sum_i H^i$. With $\bar p$ the mean price over the window, the regime is
\begin{equation}
\text{Regime}=
\begin{cases}
\text{Competitive} & \bar H<H^\ast \text{ and } \bar p<p^\ast,\\
\text{Collusive} & \bar H<H^\ast \text{ and } \bar p\ge p^\ast,\\
\text{Chaotic} & \bar H\ge H^\ast,
\end{cases}
\qquad H^\ast=1.0,\ p^\ast=2.0.
\label{eq:rule}
\end{equation}
The logic is that entropy distinguishes \emph{converged} play (low $\bar H$, mass on one or two prices)
from \emph{non-convergent} play (high $\bar H$, mass spread across many prices); conditional on
convergence, the mean price separates competitive from collusive. Figure~\ref{fig:map} shows the $100$
baseline runs in the $(\bar H,\bar p)$ plane: a low-entropy collusive cluster and a high-entropy chaotic
cluster are cleanly separated by $H^\ast$.

\section{Baseline Results}\label{sec:baseline}
Under the baseline duopoly, $56\%$ ($\pm5.0$) of runs are classified collusive and $44\%$ chaotic;
competitive convergence essentially never occurs (Figure~\ref{fig:freq}). The mean collusion index is
$\Delta=0.43$: converged runs sit roughly half-way between marginal-cost pricing and joint monopoly. The
distribution of mean prices (Figure~\ref{fig:dist}) is bimodal, with a converged collusive mass at high
prices and a diffuse chaotic mass at intermediate prices, and almost no mass at marginal cost. Two
substantive points follow. Collusion is the single most common \emph{converged} outcome, vindicating the
concern that homogeneity does not immunise a market against algorithmic coordination. Yet non-convergence
is nearly as likely as collusion: in the homogeneous limit the learning dynamics frequently fail to settle
at all, a possibility largely absent from the differentiated-good narrative.

\section{Q-learning versus Mean-Based Learning}\label{sec:qvm}
Holding the environment fixed, the stateless mean-based bandit colludes in $89\%$ of runs, against $56\%$
for $Q$-learning (Figure~\ref{fig:qvm}). The difference is large and statistically decisive: a
two-proportion test gives $z=5.23$ and $p=1.7\times10^{-7}$. The direction is the opposite of the natural
hypothesis that algorithmic sophistication causes collusion. The mean-based learner, valuing each price by
its unconditional historical average, settles quickly onto the price that has paid best under symmetric
play---a supra-competitive level---and remains there. The $Q$-learner, by contrast, bootstraps from its own
evolving value estimates over a larger state space, which makes convergence harder and leaves a greater
share of runs chaotic.

The implication for policy is uncomfortable: supra-competitive coordination does not require firms to
deploy advanced reinforcement learning. A simple bandit not only suffices but coordinates \emph{more}
reliably. The locus of concern is therefore independent payoff optimisation in general, not any particular
class of algorithm.

\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.58\textwidth]{fig5_q_vs_mean.png}
\caption{Regime frequencies under $Q$-learning and mean-based learning ($100$ runs each). The collusion
gap is significant at $z=5.23$ ($p<10^{-6}$).}\label{fig:qvm}
\end{figure}

\section{The Reward--Punishment Mechanism}\label{sec:mech}
Is collusive convergence supported by a strategic punishment of deviations, or merely by both agents
independently favouring high prices? To distinguish these I run a frozen-policy deviation experiment.
Taking each converged collusive run, I freeze the learned $Q$-tables (greedy play, no further learning),
verify steady collusive pricing, then force one firm to undercut to the lowest price for a single period
and trace the subsequent path. Averaging over $40$ collusive runs (Figure~\ref{fig:impulse}), the rival
responds to the undercut by cutting its own price---from a pre-deviation level of $5.02$ to a trough of
$4.58$---before prices recover to the collusive level ($5.04$). The pattern is a genuine, if shallow,
reward--punishment-and-recovery strategy: deviation is met with retaliation that erodes its profitability,
after which cooperation is restored. The modest depth of punishment is consistent with the homogeneous
environment's hostility to coordination and with the fragility documented below.

\begin{figure}[t]\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.66\textwidth]{fig9_impulse.png}
\caption{Average response to a forced one-period undercut at $t=0$ under frozen converged policies
($40$ collusive runs). The rival punishes (price falls) and cooperation then recovers.}\label{fig:impulse}
\end{figure}

\section{Patience and the Intensity of Collusion}\label{sec:patience}
The discount factor $\gamma$ measures how heavily an agent weighs future payoffs, and hence its willingness
to forgo a one-period undercut to preserve an ongoing arrangement. Consistent with the folk theorem
\citep{tirole1988}, the intensity of collusion rises monotonically with patience: the mean collusion index
climbs from $\Delta=0.23$ at $\gamma=0.20$ to $\Delta=0.45$ at $\gamma=0.95$ (Figure~\ref{fig:gamma} and
Table~\ref{tab:sens}). Impatient agents still coordinate frequently but only weakly, and $\gamma=0.20$ is
the sole setting in which the competitive regime appears with non-trivial probability; patient agents
collude more intensely but, pursuing higher and more fragile prices, also fall into chaos more often. The
$Q$-learner thus reproduces folk-theorem comparative statics through adaptive search, with $\gamma$
playing the role of patience, despite having no representation of repeated-game strategy.

\includegraphics[width=0.56\textwidth]{fig8_gamma_delta.png}
\caption{Collusion index $\Delta$ as a function of the discount factor $\gamma$ (patience).}\label{fig:gamma}
\end{figure}

\emph{Grid granularity.} The collusive share falls from $57\%$ at $K=7$ to $3\%$ at $K=41$. A finer grid
enlarges the state--action space faster than agents can learn it within the horizon, so the dominant
outcome becomes chaotic. This is the central reason a naive ``fine grid, short horizon'' specification
finds essentially no collusion, and it cautions against reading any single parameterisation as canonical.

\emph{Exploration.} Annealing is necessary for stable collusion. With constant $\varepsilon=0.10$ the
collusive share is only $13\%$, and with $\varepsilon=0.30$ it is zero: persistent perturbation repeatedly
destabilises incipient arrangements. The natural tendency of an efficient algorithm to reduce exploration
as it grows confident is therefore precisely the feature that enables collusion---and a mandated
exploration floor is a candidate pro-competitive intervention that a regulator could in principle audit.

\emph{Horizon.} Collusion rises monotonically with the horizon, from $10\%$ at $T=5{,}000$ to $77\%$ at
$T=60{,}000$, confirming that short-horizon specifications understate collusion because learning has not
converged.

\emph{Market structure.} Adding a third firm collapses collusion entirely ($57\%\to0\%$; all runs chaotic;
Figure~\ref{fig:three}). The joint state space grows combinatorially and an undercutting firm now captures
the market from two rivals, so coordination becomes far more fragile. A representative three-firm run is
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:threerun}.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}
Three implications follow. First, the Bertrand paradox is not robust to adaptive learning even with
homogeneous goods: payoff-maximising algorithms reach supra-competitive prices in a majority of converged
runs without communication or instruction. Second, because the \emph{simpler} learner colludes more, the
phenomenon is not a quirk of advanced reinforcement learning but a generic tendency of independent
optimisation---broadening rather than narrowing the policy concern. Third, in the homogeneous limit
collusion is nonetheless fragile and condition-dependent: it requires a tractable action space, annealed
exploration, a long horizon, and few firms, and collapses otherwise. The differentiated-good alarm of
\citet{calvano2020} is thus best read as an upper bound on the ease of algorithmic collusion.

For enforcement, the conduct-based framework that requires proof of an agreement is ill-suited to a setting
in which coordination emerges from independent learning with no communication
\citep{harrington2018,ezrachi2016}; an effects-based posture is more appropriate. My comparative statics
point to observable, structural levers: exploration floors (which I find directly prevent convergence to
collusion), market-structure thresholds (collusion vanishes at three firms), and limits on the granularity
of admissible price grids.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The analysis is computational and offers no convergence proofs; multi-agent
$Q$-learning is known to cycle rather than converge in general \citep{waltman2008}, which my chaotic
regime reflects. I restrict attention to homogeneous goods, inelastic demand, tabular learners, and
memory-one state; richer demand systems, deep reinforcement learning, longer memory, and asymmetric costs
or algorithms are natural extensions. Reported regime frequencies depend on the thresholds $(H^\ast,p^\ast)$
in \eqref{eq:rule}; I therefore emphasise the comparative statics, which are robust to moderate threshold
variation, over any single frequency. Finally, the baseline employs a coarse price grid to ensure
convergence within a feasible horizon; the grid-sensitivity results show that finer grids do not overturn
the qualitative findings but do require correspondingly longer horizons.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conc}
Implementing faithful $Q$-learning and a mean-based benchmark in homogeneous-good Bertrand competition, I
find that supra-competitive collusion does emerge---in a majority of converged duopoly runs, at about half
the monopoly markup---but that it is conditional rather than universal, that it does not require
algorithmic sophistication (a simple bandit colludes significantly more), that it is supported by a modest
reward--punishment strategy, that its intensity tracks patience as the folk theorem predicts, and that it
collapses under fine price grids, persistent exploration, short horizons, or additional firms. Tacit
algorithmic collusion is thus simultaneously more accessible and more fragile than a reading of the
differentiated-good literature alone would suggest---a combination that is, on balance, encouraging for the
design of competition policy, because the conditions on which it depends are largely observable.
